Sunderwaala Burj

History



Post-1911, Maulvi Zafar Hassan meticulously documented Delhi's monuments, laying the foundation for his book, Monuments of Dehli. Sunderwaala Burj, named for its Persian roots where "Burj" means a huge building, earned the moniker "sunder" or beautiful due to its exquisite decoration and calligraphy.

Adhering to Islamic principles, the monument abstains from human depictions, featuring instead flower motifs creating an optical illusion.

Arabic calligraphy adorns the walls, depicting the verse-surya juma of the Quran. Islamic tradition dictates that human burials should occur beneath the open sky, and as a manifestation of this belief, Sunder Burj features a celestial-themed star ceiling within its confines. Multiple jalis ensure privacy and ventilation inside the structure.

Sunder Burj is adorned with a water channel inspired by the Persian Garden concept, seamlessly blending a bird's-eye view of the traditional four-part garden (chahar bagh) with graceful profiles of birds and trees. The architectural design of the water channel is meticulously crafted to evoke a soothing wave pattern, creating a sound effect.
